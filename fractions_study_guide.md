# Mastering Fractions: A Complete Study Guide

## Introduction

Fractions are one of the most important mathematical concepts you'll encounter. They represent parts of a whole and appear everywhere—from cooking recipes to measuring distances to splitting costs with friends. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know about fractions, from the basics to advanced operations.

## What Are Fractions?

A fraction represents a part of a whole. It consists of two numbers:

- **Numerator** (top number): How many parts you have
- **Denominator** (bottom number): How many equal parts the whole is divided into

For example, in the fraction 3/4 (three-fourths):
- The numerator is 3 (you have 3 parts)
- The denominator is 4 (the whole is divided into 4 equal parts)

### Types of Fractions

**Proper Fractions**: The numerator is smaller than the denominator (1/2, 3/4, 5/8). These represent less than one whole.

**Improper Fractions**: The numerator is larger than or equal to the denominator (5/4, 7/3, 8/8). These represent one whole or more.

**Mixed Numbers**: A combination of a whole number and a proper fraction (2 1/2, 3 3/4). These also represent more than one whole.

## Converting Between Fractions and Mixed Numbers

**Converting Improper Fractions to Mixed Numbers:**
1. Divide the numerator by the denominator
2. The whole number is your quotient
3. The remainder becomes the new numerator
4. Keep the same denominator

Example: Convert 11/4 to a mixed number
- 11 ÷ 4 = 2 remainder 3
- Answer: 2 3/4

**Converting Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions:**
1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator
2. Add the numerator to this result
3. Place this number over the original denominator

Example: Convert 2 3/4 to an improper fraction
- (2 × 4) + 3 = 11
- Answer: 11/4

## Simplifying Fractions

A fraction is in its simplest form when the numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1. To simplify:

1. Find the greatest common factor (GCF) of both numbers
2. Divide both the numerator and denominator by the GCF

Example: Simplify 12/18
- Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
- Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18
- GCF = 6
- 12 ÷ 6 = 2, 18 ÷ 6 = 3
- Answer: 2/3

## Comparing Fractions

To determine which fraction is larger:

**Same Denominator**: Simply compare numerators. 3/8 > 2/8

**Different Denominators**: Find a common denominator, then compare.

Example: Compare 1/3 and 1/4
- Common denominator: 12
- 1/3 = 4/12
- 1/4 = 3/12
- Therefore: 1/3 > 1/4

## Adding and Subtracting Fractions

**Same Denominator:**
1. Add or subtract the numerators
2. Keep the denominator the same
3. Simplify if needed

Example: 2/5 + 1/5 = 3/5

**Different Denominators:**
1. Find a common denominator
2. Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions
3. Add or subtract the numerators
4. Simplify if needed

Example: 1/3 + 1/4
- Common denominator: 12
- 1/3 = 4/12, 1/4 = 3/12
- 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12

## Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying fractions is simpler than adding them:

1. Multiply the numerators together
2. Multiply the denominators together
3. Simplify the result

Example: 2/3 × 3/4
- (2 × 3)/(3 × 4) = 6/12 = 1/2

**Tip**: You can simplify before multiplying to make the numbers easier to work with.

## Dividing Fractions

Division of fractions uses a special rule: multiply by the reciprocal.

1. Keep the first fraction the same
2. Change the division sign to multiplication
3. Flip the second fraction (find its reciprocal)
4. Multiply as usual
5. Simplify

Example: 2/3 ÷ 1/4
- 2/3 × 4/1 = 8/3 = 2 2/3

## Real-World Applications

**Cooking**: A recipe calls for 2/3 cup of flour, but you want to make half the recipe. You need: 2/3 × 1/2 = 1/3 cup

**Sharing**: You have a pizza cut into 8 slices. You eat 3 slices. Your friend eats 2 slices. Together you ate: 3/8 + 2/8 = 5/8 of the pizza

**Measurements**: A board is 5 1/2 feet long. You cut off 1 3/4 feet. Remaining length: 5 1/2 - 1 3/4 = 3 3/4 feet

## Practice Problems

1. Simplify: 15/25
2. Convert to a mixed number: 17/5
3. Add: 2/3 + 1/6
4. Multiply: 3/4 × 2/5
5. Divide: 5/6 ÷ 2/3

## Conclusion

Fractions are a fundamental skill that opens doors to more advanced mathematics. By mastering the concepts of simplifying, adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions, you'll build a strong foundation for algebra, geometry, and beyond. Practice regularly, and soon working with fractions will become second nature.
