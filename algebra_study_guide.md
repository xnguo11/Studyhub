# Introduction to Algebra: Mastering Variables and Equations

## Introduction

Algebra is a branch of mathematics that uses symbols and letters (called variables) to represent unknown numbers. Instead of just working with known numbers like in arithmetic, algebra allows you to work with unknown quantities and find solutions. This powerful tool opens doors to advanced mathematics and is essential for science, engineering, economics, and countless real-world applications. This guide will introduce you to the fundamental concepts of algebra and teach you how to solve equations like a mathematician.

## What Is Algebra?

Algebra is the study of mathematical symbols and the rules for manipulating those symbols. The key innovation in algebra is the use of variables—letters that represent unknown numbers.

**Why is algebra important?**
- Solve real-world problems involving unknown quantities
- Express patterns and relationships mathematically
- Build skills for advanced mathematics like geometry and calculus
- Develop logical and critical thinking skills

## Variables and Expressions

### Variables

A variable is a letter or symbol that represents an unknown number. Common variables include x, y, z, a, and b.

**Example:** In the expression 2x + 5, the letter x is the variable. It represents some unknown number.

### Algebraic Expressions

An algebraic expression is a combination of variables, numbers, and operation symbols. It does not have an equals sign.

**Examples:**
- 3x + 7 (three times x, plus seven)
- 2y - 5 (two times y, minus five)
- a + b + 10 (a plus b plus ten)
- x² + 3x - 2 (x squared plus three x minus two)

### Terms and Coefficients

An algebraic expression is made up of terms, which are separated by + or - signs.

**Coefficient**: The number multiplied by the variable.

Example: In the expression 5x + 3y - 8
- The term 5x has a coefficient of 5
- The term 3y has a coefficient of 3
- The term -8 is a constant (a term with no variable)

## The Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

When simplifying algebraic expressions, follow the order of operations:

1. **P**arentheses: Solve operations inside parentheses first
2. **E**xponents: Calculate powers and square roots
3. **M**ultiplication and **D**ivision: Work from left to right
4. **A**ddition and **S**ubtraction: Work from left to right

**Helpful acronym:** PEMDAS (Please Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally)

Example: Simplify 2 + 3 × 4 - 1
- Multiplication first: 3 × 4 = 12
- Then left to right: 2 + 12 - 1 = 13

## Simplifying Algebraic Expressions

To simplify means to combine like terms and make the expression as simple as possible.

### Combining Like Terms

Like terms are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. You can add or subtract like terms.

Example: Simplify 3x + 5x - 2
- Combine like terms: 3x + 5x = 8x
- Result: 8x - 2

Example: Simplify 4y + 3x - 2y + 7
- Combine y terms: 4y - 2y = 2y
- Result: 2y + 3x + 7

## Equations: The Foundation of Algebra

An equation is a mathematical statement showing that two expressions are equal. It contains an equals sign (=).

**Examples:**
- x + 5 = 12
- 2x - 3 = 7
- 3x + 2 = 5x - 4

### Solutions

The solution to an equation is the value of the variable that makes the equation true.

Example: In x + 5 = 12
- If x = 7, then 7 + 5 = 12 ✓ (This is true!)
- So x = 7 is the solution

## Solving One-Step Equations

A one-step equation can be solved with a single operation.

### Using Inverse Operations

To solve equations, use inverse operations—operations that undo each other:
- Addition is undone by subtraction
- Subtraction is undone by addition
- Multiplication is undone by division
- Division is undone by multiplication

**Rule:** Whatever you do to one side of an equation, you must do to the other side.

**Example 1: Addition**
x + 8 = 15
- Subtract 8 from both sides
- x + 8 - 8 = 15 - 8
- x = 7

**Example 2: Subtraction**
x - 5 = 12
- Add 5 to both sides
- x - 5 + 5 = 12 + 5
- x = 17

**Example 3: Multiplication**
3x = 21
- Divide both sides by 3
- 3x ÷ 3 = 21 ÷ 3
- x = 7

**Example 4: Division**
x ÷ 4 = 6
- Multiply both sides by 4
- (x ÷ 4) × 4 = 6 × 4
- x = 24

## Solving Two-Step Equations

Two-step equations require two operations to solve.

**Strategy:** Work backward from the equals sign, undoing operations in reverse order of PEMDAS.

**Example:** Solve 2x + 5 = 13
1. Subtract 5 from both sides: 2x + 5 - 5 = 13 - 5
2. Simplify: 2x = 8
3. Divide both sides by 2: 2x ÷ 2 = 8 ÷ 2
4. Solution: x = 4

**Verification:** 2(4) + 5 = 8 + 5 = 13 ✓

**Example:** Solve 3x - 7 = 11
1. Add 7 to both sides: 3x - 7 + 7 = 11 + 7
2. Simplify: 3x = 18
3. Divide both sides by 3: 3x ÷ 3 = 18 ÷ 3
4. Solution: x = 6

## Solving Multi-Step Equations

Equations with more steps follow the same principles.

**Example:** Solve 4x - 3 = 2x + 9
1. Get variables on one side: 4x - 2x - 3 = 2x - 2x + 9
2. Simplify: 2x - 3 = 9
3. Add 3 to both sides: 2x - 3 + 3 = 9 + 3
4. Simplify: 2x = 12
5. Divide by 2: 2x ÷ 2 = 12 ÷ 2
6. Solution: x = 6

## Real-World Applications of Algebra

**Banking:** If you deposit money and earn interest, algebra helps calculate your final balance.
Example: You have $100 and earn 5% interest yearly. After x years, you have: 100 + (5x)

**Shopping:** Algebra helps calculate totals and compare prices.
Example: Shirts cost $15 each. If you buy x shirts and have a $10 discount: Total = 15x - 10

**Distance and Speed:** The formula distance = speed × time is an algebraic equation.
Example: A car travels at 60 mph for x hours. Distance covered = 60x miles

**Cooking:** Recipes often need to be scaled up or down.
Example: A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour. To make x times the recipe, you need: 2x cups
